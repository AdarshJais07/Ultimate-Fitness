Chest Workouts

Learn More

About

There are actually two main chest muscles: the pectoralis major and the pectoralis minor. The pectoralis major is the larger, more superficial muscle that gives your chest its definition. It helps you move your arms in several directions, including pushing and pulling motions. The pectoralis minor is a smaller muscle located beneath the pectoralis major. It helps stabilize the shoulder blade.

Back Workouts

Learn More

About

The back muscles are a large group of muscles that support your spine, posture, and movement. They help you bend, twist, and extend your back, and they also play a role in breathing. Some of the major back muscles include the latissimus dorsi (lats), the trapezius, and the rhomboids.

Shoulder Workouts

Learn More

About

The shoulder joint is surrounded by a complex group of muscles that allow for a wide range of motion, from reaching overhead to rotating your arm. These muscles can be broadly categorized into three groups: the deltoids (shoulder cap), rotator cuff (stabilizes the shoulder joint), and the rotators (move your arm).

Biceps Workouts

Learn More

About

The biceps brachii, commonly called the biceps, is an upper arm muscle with two heads. It's responsible for bending the elbow and supinating the forearm, which allows you to rotate your palm upwards. Strong biceps contribute to powerful pulling motions and give your upper arms a defined, rounded look.

Triceps Workouts

Learn More

About

The triceps, your horseshoe-shaped muscle on the back of your upper arm, is the opposite of the biceps. While the biceps bend your elbow, the triceps straighten it, allowing you to push, throw, and extend your arm. Triceps exercises like push-ups and dips help build arm strength and definition.

Legs Workouts

Learn More

About

Your legs contain several major muscle groups that work together for movement, stability, and power. Here are the main ones:

  • Quadriceps (front thigh): Extend the knee and help stabilize the kneecap.
  • Calves (lower leg): Plantarflexion (pointing toes down) essential for walking, running, and jumping.
  • Glutes (buttocks): Extend the hip, rotate the thigh outward, and help stabilize the pelvis.

Abs Workouts

Learn More

About

Your abs, short for abdominal muscles, are a group of muscles that run along your torso, supporting your spine and helping you move. They include the rectus abdominis (the "six-pack"), obliques (on the sides), and transverse abdominis (deepest layer). Strong abs improve posture, stability, and protect your organs.
